Overview

Taking MongoDB Public

When MongoDB prepared to go public on NASDAQ, they needed a creative campaign that could translate the technical power of a developer-first database platform into a compelling public narrative — one that resonated with Wall Street investors, enterprise buyers, and the broader tech community simultaneously.

As Creative Director, I was embedded with the executive leadership team — working directly with the CEO, CTO, and CMO — to develop the messaging framework and visual identity that would define MongoDB's IPO moment.

Challenge

Translating Technology Into a Public Story

MongoDB's strength lived in developer communities — deeply technical, code-first, and skeptical of marketing polish. The IPO required speaking to a much broader, mixed audience without alienating the developer base that made the product what it was.

The campaign needed to feel credible to engineers, compelling to enterprise buyers, and accessible to public market investors — all at once.

Approach

Messaging Built From the C-Suite Down

Working in close collaboration with the CEO, CTO, and CMO, I facilitated a messaging architecture process that anchored the campaign in MongoDB's genuine strengths: developer velocity, enterprise scale, and the flexibility that traditional databases couldn't offer.

From that foundation, I developed the visual system — spanning outdoor formats across major financial districts, a television commercial, digital display advertising, and a suite of print materials — all designed to feel like a coherent, confident statement rather than a marketing push.

Outcome

A Defining Moment for the Brand

The MongoDB IPO launched on October 19, 2017. The campaign ran across Times Square and San Francisco's financial district, national television, and coordinated digital channels. MongoDB priced at $24 per share and surged on its first day of trading, opening 34% above its IPO price.

The visual language developed for the IPO became a cornerstone of MongoDB's broader brand evolution over the years that followed.
